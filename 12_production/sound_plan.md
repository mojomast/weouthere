## Sound Philosophy

This show lives or dies on intelligibility. The comedy is language-driven, timing-sensitive, and often layered with hesitation, overlap, muttering, and device interaction. Production sound has to preserve nuance, not just words.

## Core Requirements

- clean lead dialogue capture on all principal scenes
- record usable room tone for every recurring space
- capture device interaction references when they matter to timing
- preserve metadata and track naming cleanly for post
- do not choose impressive-looking locations that destroy dialogue

## Production Sound Priorities

### Priority 1: Lead Dialogue

- boom-first whenever feasible
- lav support when blocking, wardrobe, or frame requires it
- protect performance rhythm over overly ambitious noisy coverage

### Priority 2: Screen-Native Action

- capture keyboard, mouse, touchpad, notification, and device handling references when story-relevant
- record call/playback reference audio when scenes are built around screens
- distinguish between guide audio and intended final production audio in reports

### Priority 3: Ambience And World Texture

- room tone for each recurring set
- wild tracks for HVAC-heavy or public spaces
- practical environment textures for office, hallway, cafe, parking, and threshold scenes

## Location Sound Rules

- any location with uncontrollable HVAC, street bleed, or reflective harshness must be challenged early
- prioritize interiors that allow repeatability over visually flashy but sonically broken spaces
- avoid stacking dialogue-heavy scenes into the noisiest practicals

## Sound Package Assumptions

- experienced production sound mixer
- boom operator on full live-action days
- adequate wireless count for the standard cast configuration
- timecode and report discipline sufficient for fast editorial turnover

## Desktop / Screen Sequence Notes

Desktop realism scenes create special sound needs:

- cursor and typing behavior affect comedic rhythm
- in-world calls may need clean local performance records and remote references
- UI sounds should usually be designed in post, but set must capture timing references
- if cast responds to playback, the source playback version must be logged

## Wild Line Policy

Record wild lines on the day when:

- typing or clicking masks critical words
- a practical location has transient noise risk
- a screen-timed beat requires exact sync language
- a lead's improvisation created a must-keep line in compromised conditions

Wild lines are a support tool, not permission to accept poor production sound.

## Handoff To Post

Sound turnover should include:

- sound reports
- track naming legend
- notes on compromised takes
- notes on playback use
- wild line log
- room tone inventory

## Sound Risks Specific To This Show

- noisy practical tech locations sold on vibe rather than usability
- wardrobe choices fighting lav placement
- screens and keyboards creating inconsistent prop noise across takes
- interface scenes requiring precision timing without enough reference capture
- late discovery that an important call scene has unclear playback provenance

## Department Coordination Notes

### AD

- protect sound time during tech scouts and first setup in new locations
- do not treat room tone as optional

### Wardrobe

- coordinate fabrics, layers, and jewelry with lav needs

### Props / Art

- test keyboards, chairs, bags, and desk objects for unwanted noise signatures

### Post

- flag early if production track patterns suggest ADR risk building

## ADR Threshold

ADR should be reserved for:

- unintelligible key story information
- broken sync in screen-critical sequences
- unavoidable environmental destruction of must-keep performance

ADR should not become the plan for fast dialogue scenes that could have been protected on set.
