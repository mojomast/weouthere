## Staffing Assumptions

This plan assumes:

- WE OUT HERE is a 6 x 30 minute indie professional series
- development work happens mostly remotely
- prep and production are tightly coordinated through a lean on-the-ground team
- post combines remote editorial work with scheduled review and finishing sessions
- most hires are project-based employees or contractors, not permanent staff

## Staffing Goals

The staffing plan is designed to:

- protect core creative leadership
- attach experienced department heads early enough to avoid amateur mistakes
- keep payroll and contractor complexity manageable
- create clear hiring gates tied to budget and schedule readiness
- reduce burnout by staffing for real workload, not heroic assumptions

## Phase-Based Staffing Plan

### Phase 1: Development and Packaging

| Role | Target Timing | Engagement Type | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Executive Producer / Producing Lead | Immediate | Principal attachment | Business lead and approval authority |
| Showrunner / Creator | Immediate | Principal attachment | Creative lead |
| Head Writer or Writers' Room Lead | Early development | Part-time or weekly | Needed if room support extends beyond founder writing |
| Line Producer | Early development | Fractional consulting, then weekly | Needed before serious hiring or scheduling |
| Casting Director | Packaging stage | Part-time project engagement | Starts once breakdowns and budget lane are credible |
| Bookkeeping / Payroll Support | Early development | Fractional | Avoids messy records and delayed setup |
| Legal and HR admin support | Early development | Fractional | Agreement handling, intake, compliance tracking |

| --- | --- | --- | --- |

| Production Manager | Start of prep | Full-time during prep and shoot | Owns logistics and vendor movement |
| Production Coordinator | Start of prep | Full-time during prep and shoot | Required once active hiring and paperwork increase |
| 1st Assistant Director | Mid-prep | Weekly then full shoot | Builds schedule and set cadence |
| Director of Photography | Mid-prep | Prep + shoot deal | Camera tests, gear, visual approach |
| Production Designer | Mid-prep | Prep + shoot deal | Locations, props, set planning |

| Hair and Makeup Lead | Mid-prep | Prep + shoot deal | Look planning and on-set execution |

| Post Supervisor | Late prep | Part-time then heavier in post | Important for planning media and deliverables early |

### Phase 3: Production

| Team Area | Expected Staffing |
| --- | --- |
| Production office | Line Producer, Production Manager, Production Coordinator, office PA if needed |
| Direction / schedule | Director, 1st AD, 2nd AD or set PA support depending on schedule density |

| Role | Timing | Engagement Type | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Editor | Start during production or immediately after | Weekly | Core post hire |
| Assistant Editor | Once media volume justifies it | Weekly | Required for clean turnovers and backups |

## Minimum Viable Team by Phase

If budget tightens, do not cut these roles below minimum:

| Phase | Minimum team |
| --- | --- |
| Development | Showrunner, EP, Line Producer, bookkeeping support |
| Prep | Production Manager, Coordinator, Director, DP, Production Designer, Casting Director |
| Shoot | 1st AD, DP, Sound Mixer, key production office staff, essential art and safety coverage |
| Post | Editor, Post Supervisor, sound finish, color finish |

## Priority Hiring Order

### Tier 1: Must hire before broad recruiting

- Line Producer
- Production Manager
- Casting Director
- HR and paperwork support

### Tier 2: Must hire before locking the schedule

- Director
- 1st AD
- DP
- Production Designer
- Post Supervisor

### Tier 3: Must hire before first day of principal photography

- Coordinator
- Sound Mixer
- Costume and Hair/Makeup leads
- Script Supervisor
- Core camera, grip, electric, and PA support

## Engagement Model

Use the simplest legal structure that matches the work.

- Principals and company decision-makers are attached by negotiated producing agreements.
- Some cast and crew may need payroll employee treatment depending on jurisdiction, union status, and shoot structure.
- Specialist collaborators such as composers, designers, editors, and some consultants may be engaged as contractors if lawful and appropriate.
- No one starts work without a signed agreement, rate confirmation, and a defined reporting line.

## Offer Design Guidelines

Every offer should include:

- role title
- reporting line
- dates or expected engagement window
- pay structure and overtime rules if applicable
- scope of work
- credit language if applicable
- required paperwork and start conditions
- workplace policy acknowledgment requirements

## Hiring Gates

### Gate 1: Packaging Ready

Before attaching most paid collaborators:

- creative materials are coherent enough to pitch the project honestly
- the budget lane is real, not aspirational fiction
- the entity and rights posture are clean enough to contract people

### Gate 2: Prep Ready

Before hiring most department heads:

- the production window is reasonably defined
- financing for prep is approved
- the Line Producer confirms workload and staffing assumptions

### Gate 3: Shoot Ready

Before confirming daily crew calls:

- schedule, locations, and insurance assumptions are active
- payroll, onboarding, and safety systems are functioning
- department heads confirm they have enough crew to work safely

## Staffing Risk Triggers

Add support immediately if any of the following happen:

- Production Manager is carrying both coordinator and office management work for more than 1 week
- department heads report repeated missed prep deadlines due to understaffing
- edit team is falling more than 5 business days behind footage intake
- regular workweeks exceed 55 hours in prep or post for more than 2 weeks
- more than 3 active candidate searches are being run without recruiting coordination support

## Staffing Philosophy

- Pay for competence before paying for volume.
- Hire people who can work well in hybrid conditions and communicate clearly.
- Avoid unpaid trial work.
- Do not ask one person to quietly cover two full jobs unless the agreement and compensation reflect it.
- A smaller senior crew is usually safer than a larger inexperienced crew.
