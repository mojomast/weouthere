## Why This Matters

Indie projects often burn people out by pretending that commitment can replace staffing, planning, or recovery time. WE OUT HERE should not run on chronic overextension.

Burnout prevention is not just a wellness topic. It protects:

- creative quality
- safety
- retention
- schedule reliability
- decision-making under pressure

## Main Burnout Risks For This Project

- remote development that quietly turns into always-on work
- too few production office staff in prep
- compressed shoot days with repeated overtime
- post schedules that assume endless revision capacity
- unclear approval chains that create last-minute thrash
- emotional labor falling repeatedly on the same people

## Baseline Guardrails

### Remote development and post

- default to normal working-hour communication unless urgent
- do not expect same-night turnaround on non-urgent notes
- keep recurring meetings to the minimum needed for coordination
- publish deadlines with at least 24 hours of context when possible
- use async notes for simple review items instead of adding meetings

### Prep

- flag any recurring workweek above 55 hours
- if a coordinator or manager is handling two full roles for more than 1 week, add support or cut scope
- protect at least one no-meeting block per week for deep work in heavy planning periods

### Production

- schedule realistic company moves and turnaround time
- avoid normalizing rolling meal penalties and repeated late changes
- department heads must flag unsafe or unsustainable days before crew call, not only after damage is done
- if repeated overtime becomes expected rather than exceptional, rework staffing or schedule

### Post

- lock note rounds and review windows where possible
- avoid endless same-day recuts unless tied to delivery emergencies
- maintain a visible cut calendar so editors are not absorbing surprise urgency alone

## Meeting and Response Norms

- no meeting should exist without a decision, review, or coordination purpose
- use agendas for meetings longer than 20 minutes
- avoid turning chat into a second full-time workplace after hours
- urgent means time-sensitive to safety, schedule, or delivery, not just anxiety

## Staffing Triggers

Add labor, not just pressure, when these appear:

- more than 2 consecutive weeks above the agreed workload range
- repeated missed deadlines caused by insufficient staffing, not poor performance
- leaders spending large amounts of time on admin work that should be delegated
- editors, coordinators, or department heads becoming bottlenecks for basic information flow
- quality drops caused by exhaustion, version confusion, or avoidable mistakes

## Recovery Practices

- schedule buffer days where possible between major production and post milestones
- provide comp time or recovery time when workloads spike beyond plan
- avoid stacking major creative review sessions late at night unless truly necessary
- encourage people to speak up before they are at a breaking point

## Manager Responsibilities

Managers and department heads must:

- watch for signs of overload, withdrawal, irritability, and repeated errors
- ask direct workload questions instead of assuming silence means capacity
- escalate staffing and schedule risks early
- not reward burnout behavior as proof of loyalty

## Individual Expectations

Team members are expected to:

- communicate capacity issues early
- track deadlines honestly
- not create false urgency for poor planning
- respect other people's off-hours unless there is a real need

## Weekly Pulse Check

During active prep, production, and post, managers should ask:

1. What is at risk this week?
2. Where is work piling up?
3. Who is carrying invisible labor?
4. What can be clarified, delegated, or cut?

## Escalation Path

- workload concern -> direct manager
- unresolved workload concern -> Production Manager or Post Supervisor
- systemic burnout risk -> Line Producer and EP
- if workload is tied to disrespect, retaliation, or unsafe conditions -> use policy reporting routes immediately

## Simple Rule

If the plan only works when everyone quietly over-functions, the plan is wrong.
