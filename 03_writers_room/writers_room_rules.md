# 03_writers_room/writers_room_rules.md

**Purpose:** Define room principles, tone rules, taboo shortcuts, and continuity process for writing WE OUT HERE.

**Owner/Subagent:** OpenCode

**Status:** Draft v1

**Dependencies:** `03_writers_room/episode_beat_sheets.md`, `03_writers_room/scene_cards.md`, `03_writers_room/defense_mechanisms_tracker.md`, `03_writers_room/continuity_matrix.md`, `03_writers_room/emotional_arc_map.md`

## Room Principles

1. The comedy comes from defense mechanisms under labor pressure, not random zaniness.
2. Cataclysm Consulting must always feel competent enough to keep getting hired and broken enough to keep causing damage.
3. Backchannel is not just a tool. It is a social architecture that rewards leakage, performance, and false intimacy.
4. Every episode should turn an attempt at containment into a new exposure.
5. No lead is the moral center. All three are funny because they are adaptive, incisive, and compromised.
6. Endings preserve instability. We do not restore order, heal the wound, or fully punish the guilty.

## Tone Rules

1. Dark sitcom first, industry satire second, psychodrama always underneath.
2. Dialogue should be sharp, specific, and slightly too fluent when characters are avoiding themselves.
3. Use desktop realism for plot precision, live action for relational discomfort, surreal inserts for psychic overflow.
4. Surreal inserts must emerge from platform logic, stress, shame, or manic cognition. They cannot feel decorative.
5. The show should be sexy only in the sense that power, containment, permission, and attention become erotically charged. Do not flatten that into obvious gag writing.
6. Corporate language should be funny because it is accurate enough to hurt.

## Character Rules

### twinkle.puss

- Writes to stay ahead of feeling.
- Can be genuinely brilliant; never reduce him to chaos pixie damage.
- When he confesses, test whether the confession is also a performance grab.

### bl0ng

- The colder he gets, the funnier and scarier he should be.
- His jokes often arrive as systems language, not punchlines.
- He should never become a generic evil tech guy; his control is intimate, needy, and oddly sincere.

### ariane.emory

- She is not there to explain the theme to the audience.
- Her rigor is real, but it frequently delays action.
- She must remain complicit enough to stay dramatically inside the trio.

## Taboo Shortcuts

1. No broad parody of "dumb tech people." The characters are smart; that is part of the danger.
2. No clean villain-of-the-week structure that lets the trio off the hook.
3. No after-school-special speeches where someone finally says the correct thing and the scene agrees.
4. No magical hacker nonsense. Backchannel problems should be social, procedural, permissions-based, and plausible.
5. No trauma dumping as explanation. Pain should shape behavior, not excuse it.
6. No redemption beats that outrun the damage.
7. No meme-stuffing or generic startup buzzword soup without character or plot function.
8. No ending where a lesson is learned and operationalized into wellness.

## Episode Construction Rules

1. A story drives the crisis architecture.
2. B story should complicate control, visibility, or authorship.
3. C story carries conscience, but conscience must remain compromised.
4. Midpoint should usually be a false save, reputational upgrade, or institutional misreading of harm as innovation.
5. Final beat should create new dread, new leverage, or new circulation of damaging language.

## Continuity Process

1. Before breaking a new episode, check the screenshot trail, viewer visibility rules, and current level of Backchannel access.
2. Track which phrases have entered company vocabulary so finale language collisions feel earned.
3. Maintain client-specific consequences. Referrals, awards, and crises should grow from prior damage.
4. Reuse props and digital artifacts when they can carry guilt or absurdity forward.
5. Confirm what each lead knows, suspects, and can prove at each stage.

## Room Questions To Ask On Every Pass

1. What defense mechanism is driving this scene?
2. What labor pressure makes the bad choice plausible?
3. What exactly is being contained, and why does containment fail?
4. Is the joke revealing character and system at the same time?
5. Does the ending preserve instability rather than closure?

## Drafting Targets

- Keep scenes pressure-forward and playable.
- Favor embarrassing specificity over generalized satire.
- Let typed language and spoken language contradict each other.
- Build repeated phrases into dread mechanisms.
- Protect the season arc: mania -> fragile success -> ethical drift -> containment failure -> ironic collapse -> exposure to consequences.
