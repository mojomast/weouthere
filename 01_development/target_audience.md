## Primary Audience

### Digitally Fluent 25-44 Viewers
These viewers work in or around knowledge industries, media, tech, startups, design, consulting, academia, freelance creative labor, or adjacent white-collar environments shaped by screens and performative expertise.

Why they connect:
- they recognize the language of Backchannel, decks, DMs, and pseudo-strategy immediately
- they understand burnout as a social condition, not a buzzword
- they have firsthand experience with hype cycles, empty process, and moral compromise at work
- they respond to comedy that treats them as literate adults

## Secondary Audience

### Prestige-Comedy Viewers
Fans of smart, dark, character-driven comedies who may not care about AI specifically but respond to social precision, flawed friendships, and tonal ambition.

Why they connect:
- the friendship engine is universal
- the humor comes from character and pressure, not niche references alone
- the rise-and-collapse arc gives the season narrative momentum beyond workplace satire

### Online-Native Millennial and Older Gen Z Audiences
Viewers shaped by forum culture, group chats, meme fluency, therapeutic discourse, and the collapse of boundaries between public and private selves.

Why they connect:
- the show understands how people actually speak online
- it captures self-aware dysfunction without romanticizing it
- it turns familiar digital behavior into story consequence

## Tertiary Audience

### Industry and Media Insiders
Writers, producers, journalists, strategists, marketers, consultants, and founders who will enjoy the accuracy of the professional and rhetorical behavior.

Strategic value:
- this audience can become organic advocates because the series speaks their language without pandering
- they are likely to quote, clip, and circulate the show's most precise observations

## Audience Promise
The series promises viewers that it will:
- understand contemporary work life from the inside
- be funny without flattening mental instability into a gimmick
- dramatize AI culture as a human systems problem
- reward close listening and rewatching through layered language and screen detail

## Positioning Statement
`WE OUT HERE` is for viewers who have ever watched a group chat become a company, a crisis become a deck, or a self-aware adult talk themselves into a worse idea because everyone around them was also tired, fluent, and scared.

## Viewer Hooks
- recognizable workplace and platform behavior
- sharp, quotable language
- escalating consequences across only six episodes
- a strong trio dynamic with distinct comic functions
- current subject matter without didactic framing

## Audience Risks and Mitigations

### Risk: Too niche for non-tech viewers
Mitigation: foreground friendship, status anxiety, burnout, and self-delusion in all positioning materials.

### Risk: Perceived as anti-AI issue comedy
Mitigation: emphasize character behavior and social systems rather than "message" framing.

### Risk: Misread as a broad startup spoof
Mitigation: lead with tonal comps that signal psychological precision and dark-comedy credibility.

## Programming Fit
Best fit platforms, buyers, or programming contexts are those that support:
- premium half-hour auteur comedy
- culturally current workplace material
- audience discovery through critical conversation and social clipping
- serialized emotional escalation inside a compact season order
