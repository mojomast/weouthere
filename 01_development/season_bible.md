## Series Overview

### Core Canon
- Title: `WE OUT HERE`
- Format: `6 x 30 min, single-camera dark sitcom / tech psychocomedy`
- Style: `desktop realism + live action + surreal inserts`
- Soft lock company name: `Cataclysm Consulting`

### Logline
Three burned-out technologists launch a chaotic AI consultancy out of `Backchannel` and try to brute-force legitimacy through manic hustle, irony, pseudo-legal improvisation, and memes. The thing that keeps the company operational is the same thing degrading everyone involved: a friendship structure that substitutes for management, treatment, accountability, and love.

### Premise Expansion
`WE OUT HERE` tracks the first season of Cataclysm Consulting, a maybe-company assembled in the vacuum between layoffs, untreated mood disorder, platform collapse, and the cultural normalization of synthetic language. The founders are bright enough to build decks, prompts, prototypes, and narratives on demand, but not stable enough to distinguish a business opportunity from a dissociative event. Their work is half client service, half live-action self-fiction. They do not so much found a startup as improvise one into temporary existence.

The comedy comes from precision rather than broadness. The characters are not generic "tech people". They are psychologically literate, self-mythologizing, internet-native adults who can name their patterns in therapeutic language while remaining unable to interrupt them. They speak in disclaimers, screenshots, subclauses, and memes because those forms let them act without committing to any coherent self. The show treats their behavior as funny, dangerous, adaptive, and pathetic at the same time.

### Engine
Each episode pairs:
- a concrete external business problem
- an internal psychological destabilization
- a group-chat escalation that distorts reality faster than anyone can metabolize it

The season engine is simple: Cataclysm keeps surviving just long enough to justify its own continuation. Every near-collapse produces one new credential, one new lie, and one new interpersonal wound.

### Series Promise
The series delivers:
- sharply observed workplace and internet behavior
- escalating comic disasters rooted in character, not randomness
- a precise depiction of mania-adjacent startup culture without glamorizing it
- emotionally real friendship damage beneath performative irony
- desktop-native storytelling that feels current without being gimmicky

## World Building

### The Near-Present Tech World
The show exists in a near-present media ecosystem where AI tools are widely available, poorly understood, socially overvalued, and strategically misrepresented by almost everyone using them. Nothing is far future. The absurdity comes from the existing mismatch between capability, hype, legal frameworks, and human need.

The economy is soft, the labor market is unstable, and expertise is increasingly theatrical. Decision-makers want AI because it signals relevance. Nobody wants to admit they do not know what they are buying. This creates ideal conditions for consultants who can convert confusion into language, decks, workflows, and vibes.

### Backchannel
`Backchannel` is the fictional platform where Cataclysm is born and where much of the series' strategic and emotional life unfolds.

Backchannel characteristics:
- server-based, channel-based, DM-heavy social platform
- used for work, friendship, subculture organization, and private spiral sessions
- frictionless enough to encourage impulsive posting and mission creep
- visually legible on screen without mimicking any real platform too literally
- treated as a real social architecture, not just a text-message device

Backchannel is the failed office, the group nervous system, and the archive of future evidence. A message sent there can function as a joke, a binding business decision, an intimacy bid, a threat, or a cry for help, often simultaneously.

### Cataclysm Consulting
Cataclysm Consulting is a consultancy before it is a corporation. Its operating model is held together by shared documents, borrowed confidence, and the founders' ability to infer what anxious clients want to hear.

Initial service offerings may include:
- AI strategy and adoption consulting
- prompt and workflow design
- synthetic brand voice packages
- internal tooling improvisation
- crisis messaging reframed as innovation enablement

What clients think they are buying:
- access to expertise
- future-proofing
- proprietary systems
- credibility by association with "people who get it"

What they are often actually buying:
- language that helps them postpone accountability
- theater that stabilizes leadership panic
- rapid prototyping by exhausted generalists
- someone willing to say yes before the legal team notices

### The Social Logic of the World
The characters live inside a culture where:
- over-articulation can substitute for sincerity
- self-diagnosis can become identity armor
- professionalism is increasingly cosplay
- every relationship risks becoming extractive under pressure
- visibility and legitimacy are confused for safety

The series should never drift into "look at these weird internet people." The environment is mainstream enough that the audience recognizes it immediately. The terror is that these people are not fringe. They are contemporary.

## Character Operating Frames

### twinkle.puss
twinkle.puss is the engine starter. He is volatile, magnetic, absurdly online, and highly effective at converting mood into momentum. He can read a room, a trend, a weakness, and a symbolic opportunity almost instantly. His gifts are real. So is his inability to tolerate containment.

Psychological frame:
- experiences activation as clarity
- treats acceleration as proof that something true is happening
- uses humor and semantic excess to outrun shame
- confuses audience response with interpersonal safety
- becomes most persuasive when least regulated

Dramatic use:
- starts opportunities nobody else would dare to name
- pushes every scene toward risk, commitment, or exposure
- creates both the company's charisma and its largest liabilities

### bl0ng
bl0ng is the systems brain and principal rationalizer. He presents as the person most interested in process, but his process often functions as a decorative layer over impulsive behavior. He wants to be the adult in the room and also wants the room to reward him for enabling the chaos.

Psychological frame:
- metabolizes anxiety into frameworks and caveats
- feels safest when constructing post-hoc logic
- mistakes technical competence for moral insulation
- dissociates into task orientation when stakes become emotional
- resents being needed while organizing his life around being indispensable

Dramatic use:
- turns bad ideas into implementable workflows
- notices risk early but rarely stops the machine
- supplies procedural language that lets others keep crossing lines

### ariane.emory
ariane.emory is the closest thing the trio has to conscience, but not because she is more stable in any simple sense. She is psychologically acute, rhetorically gifted, and capable of the most honest read on what is happening, including when she is participating in it. Her conflict is not ignorance; it is complicity under conditions of attachment.

Psychological frame:
- sees the interpersonal truth too quickly to relax
- uses irony as a way to remain present without surrendering belief
- understands systems of harm while still seeking special exemption for her own group
- wants to rescue others partly to avoid confronting her own ambition
- reaches for containment, then withdraws when containment requires power

Dramatic use:
- names the cost of what the company is doing
- keeps scenes emotionally grounded without flattening comedy
- becomes tragicomic when her insight fails to produce action

### Group Dynamic
The trio functions as a self-sealing emotional structure.

They provide each other:
- witness
- language
- permission
- tactical support
- plausible deniability

They fail to provide each other:
- limits
- rest
- accountability
- reality testing
- any durable distinction between care and collusion

Their friendship is the season's central machine. It is not secretly healthy underneath the jokes. It is adaptive, loving, exploitative, and unsustainable all at once.

## Detailed Season Arc By Episode

### Episode 1: `Don't Send That`

#### Story Function
Pilot and ignition event. The company begins as an impulsive response to humiliation, precarity, and the intoxicating possibility that language can create reality faster than institutions can verify it.

#### External Plot
After a destabilizing professional slight or public-facing embarrassment, the trio use Backchannel to joke about starting an AI consultancy, then accidentally cross the line from bit to business. A message, deck, or outreach draft that should not be sent gets sent. Instead of disaster, it generates interest.

#### Internal Movement
Season phase: `Mania`

All three experience the high of sudden shared purpose. The episode dramatizes how quickly shame flips into grandiosity when there is an audience and a possible win. The pilot must make clear that Cataclysm is born less from entrepreneurship than from the inability to metabolize defeat at ordinary scale.

#### Key Turns
- the "joke company" gets a real reply
- the trio assign roles retroactively to preserve the illusion of intention
- first pseudo-legal language appears to make them sound incorporated before they are meaningfully organized
- success arrives early enough to feel like cosmic confirmation

#### Episode Outcome
Cataclysm exists. The founders interpret a small opening as proof of destiny.

### Episode 2: `Terms of Abuse`

#### Story Function
The company encounters the first institutional threshold: contracts, liability, platform terms, client expectations, and the difference between sounding legitimate and being protected.

#### External Plot
A prospective client wants rapid AI implementation help, but legal ambiguity and data-use questions threaten the deal. The trio attempt to draft agreements, policies, and disclaimers themselves, repurposing templates, generated language, and vibes into a pseudo-compliance package.

#### Internal Movement
Season phase: `Fragile success`

The team experiences the thrill of being chosen alongside the panic of being discoverable. They start learning that legitimacy requires documentation, and documentation creates traceability. The more they formalize themselves, the more visible their fraudulence and instability become.

#### Key Turns
- a client asks a straightforward legal or ethical question nobody can answer cleanly
- bl0ng constructs a document stack that looks robust but depends on buried ambiguity
- twinkle.puss reframes risk as confidence theater
- ariane.emory recognizes they are not merely improvising but manufacturing permission

#### Episode Outcome
They close the deal by abusing the gap between comprehension and assent. This feels like adulthood and contamination at the same time.

### Episode 3: `The Manic Defense`

#### Story Function
The founders begin to convert pathology into doctrine. What looked like hustle now gains an ideology: speed, intuition, exceptionality, and anti-bureaucratic "genius" become moral cover for increasingly erratic conduct.

#### External Plot
Cataclysm's first meaningful deliverable is late, incoherent, or unstable. Rather than admit they are overextended, the trio construct a narrative that the client's confusion proves Cataclysm is ahead of the curve. A meeting or presentation becomes a high-wire act of improvisational self-justification.

#### Internal Movement
Season phase: `Ethical drift`

This is where the season's moral weather changes. The founders start using each other's vulnerability language strategically. The group discovers that any concern can be neutralized by reframing it as fear, normativity, or lack of vision.

#### Key Turns
- poor execution is recoded as disruptive innovation
- the trio begin protecting the company from reality rather than protecting reality from the company
- ariane.emory's objections become easier to absorb than to honor
- a client reward or positive testimonial confirms the wrong lesson

#### Episode Outcome
Cataclysm survives by making its instability look premium.

### Episode 4: `Contain Me Daddy`

#### Story Function
The season's containment fantasy surfaces explicitly. The founders know they need limits, supervision, or structure, but only on terms that preserve their exceptional self-image. Their attempt to import containment becomes eroticized, commodified, absurd, and impossible.

#### External Plot
Under mounting pressure, the trio pursue an advisor, interim operator, therapist-adjacent consultant, investor, or institutional partner who might legitimize and restrain them. Instead, they project wildly different needs onto that figure and turn the relationship into another site of manipulation.

#### Internal Movement
Season phase: `Containment failure`

This episode should make plain that the founders do not actually want ordinary governance. They want a parent who will bless the chaos while absorbing its consequences. The comedy lands because everyone can articulate the need for structure while sabotaging every structure that appears.

#### Key Turns
- the group mistakes external oversight for emotional rescue
- twinkle.puss tests limits as soon as they are proposed
- bl0ng builds interfaces around oversight that nullify it
- ariane.emory briefly believes someone else can stop the slide
- the advisor or authority figure exits, capitulates, or becomes complicit

#### Episode Outcome
The last credible container fails. After this point, the collapse is not preventable within the group's existing logic.

### Episode 5: `The Superego Collapse`

#### Story Function
Private rationalization becomes public contradiction. The founders can no longer maintain coherence between what they say internally, what they promise externally, and what they believe about themselves.

#### External Plot
A campaign, product demo, client deployment, leak, or public-facing artifact escapes the context that made it seem manageable. Stakeholders react. Screenshots circulate. Internal jokes read as evidence. The company's identity fractures across audiences.

#### Internal Movement
Season phase: `Ironic collapse`

The trio lose the protective function of irony. Statements that once floated as jokes now have consequences independent of intention. The episode should feel like a superego event: shame, accusation, self-justification, confession, and counterattack all arrive at once.

#### Key Turns
- Backchannel logs or adjacent artifacts become legible as proof
- each founder attempts a different narrative of responsibility
- friendship fractures along preexisting asymmetries of labor, charisma, and conscience
- the company's brand voice starts speaking more honestly than the founders can

#### Episode Outcome
Cataclysm is still technically operational, but its social reality is broken. Exposure is now inevitable.

### Episode 6: `Let Him Cook()`

#### Story Function
Season finale and consequence delivery system. The group tries one last escalation instead of surrender, hoping that a sufficiently bold move can convert scandal into breakthrough. It cannot.

#### External Plot
Faced with reputational damage, client instability, and possible legal or social fallout, the founders bet on a last demonstration, launch, pitch, or statement. They frame it as radical transparency, product vision, or narrative reclamation. In practice, it accelerates exposure and forces everyone around them to see the company clearly.

#### Internal Movement
Season phase: `Exposure to consequences`

The finale is not about punishment as moral tidiness. It is about consequence as contact with reality. The founders finally encounter limits that cannot be memed, litigated, or vibes-managed away. Even here, the show preserves ambiguity: collapse may be clarifying, but it is not automatically curative.

#### Key Turns
- the trio reassemble for one final coordinated act
- old comic rhythms return briefly, making the audience feel why the company lasted this long
- the move exposes the exact fault lines each character spent the season disguising
- the ending closes one cycle while leaving the characters alive inside its aftermath

#### Episode Outcome
Cataclysm's myth is punctured. The friendship endures in damaged form, stripped of some illusions and still vulnerable to rebuilding the same machine.

## Tonal Rules

### Comic Tone
- The show is funny because the characters are exact, not because the world is random.
- Humor should emerge from overconfidence, misattunement, semantic inflation, social performance, and catastrophic precision.
- Jokes can be fast and dense, but scenes must still track emotionally.
- No generic startup satire. Specificity is the comedy.

### Dramatic Tone
- Treat mood instability, burnout, panic, and shame as real conditions with real interpersonal effects.
- Never romanticize dysfunction just because it is charismatic.
- Avoid "very special episode" tonal breaks; the dark comedy should deepen under pressure rather than disappear.
- Let consequences feel material: money, labor, trust, sleep, reputation, and self-respect all erode.

### Surreal Tone
- Surreal inserts externalize cognition, not plot mechanics.
- They should represent distorted perception, intrusive fantasy, platform logic, or emotional truth.
- They must be short, legible, and motivated by point of view.
- Surrealism cannot solve story problems or replace scene work.

### Performance Tone
- Characters are highly verbal but should not sound interchangeable.
- Irony is a defense pattern, not a default joke setting.
- Emotional disclosure is often partial, tactical, or mistimed.
- When sincerity breaks through, it should be simple and slightly frightening.

## Structural Rules

### Episode Construction
Each episode should contain:
- one primary external business engine
- one internal relational fracture point
- one Backchannel escalation sequence
- one live-action set piece where private logic meets public consequence

### Desktop Realism Rules
- On-screen interface storytelling must always advance plot, character, or pressure.
- Message threads should reveal timing, omission, pile-on dynamics, and interpretation gaps.
- Desktop sequences cannot become static exposition dumps.
- Screen language should be readable and dramatically shaped.

### Scene Logic
- Enter scenes late, once desire and pressure are already active.
- Exit on reversals, exposures, commitments, or emotional evasions.
- Do not over-explain tech concepts if a character objective can carry the scene.
- If a scene can be about both a workflow problem and an attachment problem, it should be.

### Escalation Rules
- Problems should worsen because the characters double down on their adaptive style.
- Wins must create new liabilities.
- Every shortcut should leave residue.
- The season should feel cumulative; no episode fully resets the group's moral or emotional state.

### Single-Camera Rules
- The camera should observe behavior rather than editorialize it.
- Avoid broad mockumentary signaling.
- Visual language can be intimate, embarrassing, and sharply composed without becoming glossy startup fantasy.

## Thematic Architecture

### Primary Theme: Friendship as Failed Containment
The season asks what happens when friendship is forced to do jobs it cannot do: company governance, psychiatric holding, moral arbitration, class survival, and identity reinforcement. The answer is not that friendship fails because it is weak. It fails because it is overloaded.

### Secondary Theme: Legitimacy as Collective Delusion
Cataclysm works because many institutions are already performing certainty they do not possess. The protagonists are not anomalies in a sane system. They are opportunists in a culture built on formalized bluffing.

### Secondary Theme: Irony as Solvent
Irony protects the characters from humiliation, but it also dissolves agency. If everything can be disowned as a joke, nothing can be fully owned as a choice until consequences force the issue.

### Secondary Theme: Language as Infrastructure
Words are not decoration in this show. Pitches, prompts, disclaimers, policy language, apology posts, and group chat messages create actionable reality. The season should repeatedly show language functioning as architecture, camouflage, seduction, and evidence.

### Secondary Theme: Contemporary Selfhood Under Platform Pressure
The show examines what it means to live as a self that is distributed across channels, handles, documents, decks, clips, and logs. The characters are not hiding behind screens; they are partially constituted by them.

## Recurring Elements

### Formal Recurring Elements
- Backchannel threads and channel drift
- desktop capture sequences showing rapid narrative mutation
- decks, docs, notion-like planning artifacts, intake forms, and policy pages
- surreal inserts representing mood, fantasy, or cognitive distortion
- abrupt transitions from ironic banter to material stakes

### Behavioral Recurring Elements
- messages typed, deleted, then sent anyway
- last-minute role assignment before client-facing moments
- pseudo-legal hedging used as emotional anesthesia
- one founder trying to pause the machine while another accelerates it
- minor victories immediately converted into overreach

### Emotional Recurring Elements
- care expressed as enabling labor
- resentment disguised as competence
- confession arriving one beat too late to change behavior
- shame displaced into branding, language, or speed
- the group repeatedly mistaking mutual recognition for mutual repair

### Environmental Recurring Elements
- apartments functioning as offices, clinics, bunkers, and crash sites
- overlit coworking or conference spaces that expose the trio's fragility
- unstable domestic routines: food forgotten, sleep deferred, errands abandoned
- ambient evidence of digital overextension rather than flashy futurism

## Production Notes

### What Must Stay Concrete
- client asks should be understandable in ordinary language
- deliverables should be specific enough that failure has shape
- money and timeline pressure must remain legible
- legal and ethical ambiguity should be dramatized through decisions, not abstract debate

### What Must Stay Psychologically Precise
- escalation should emerge from defensible inner logic
- characters may misname their motives, but the show should not
- care and exploitation often coexist in the same action
- regulation, dysregulation, collapse, and repair attempts should be behaviorally distinct

### What To Avoid
- broad anti-tech moralizing
- sentimental redemption arcs that outrun damage
- jargon for its own sake
- portraying mental instability as magical insight without cost
- flattening clients, operators, or outsiders into idiots just to protect the leads
