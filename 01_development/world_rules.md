## Core World Rules

### 1. The World Is Near-Present, Not Futuristic
Nothing in `WE OUT HERE` requires speculative technology beyond current cultural plausibility. The show's absurdity comes from existing conditions intensified by pressure, not from magic future tools.

Practical implications:
- AI outputs can be impressive, wrong, incoherent, useful, risky, or all at once.
- Institutions overestimate what tools can do and underestimate implementation risk.
- Characters can bluff expertise because the surrounding culture is also bluffing.

### 2. Screens Are Story, Not Decoration
Desktop realism is integral to plot delivery. Messages, docs, decks, channels, comments, and system prompts often contain the actual turning point of a scene.

Execution rules:
- On-screen text must be readable and consequential.
- Screen sequences should advance choice, conflict, or evidence.
- If a screen beat can be removed without changing the story, it should be cut or rebuilt.

### 3. Backchannel Is a Social Architecture
Backchannel is not a neutral message device. It shapes how people perform, avoid, recruit, and escalate.

Backchannel rules:
- Channels create partial publics; people post to multiple audiences at once.
- DMs create false intimacy and parallel narratives.
- Pins, edits, deletions, and screenshots affect power.
- A message can be a joke, directive, flirtation, threat, and binding business move simultaneously.

### 4. Cataclysm Consulting Must Always Feel Plausible
The company cannot operate on cartoon logic. It survives because there is a real market for confidence, framing, and expedited ambiguity.

Business rules:
- Clients hire Cataclysm because they are anxious, time-poor, status-sensitive, or organizationally confused.
- Cataclysm often sells language and process before durable technical capability.
- The founders are capable enough to deliver partial value, which is why they remain dangerous rather than harmless.

### 5. The Characters Are Smart
Nobody is dumb for the sake of comedy. Errors come from self-interest, overconfidence, dysregulation, attachment, and institutional pressure.

Writing rule:
- Avoid easy jokes built on ignorance when a better joke can come from motivated misreading or strategic avoidance.

### 6. Psychological States Are Real, Not Quirks
Mania-adjacent activation, burnout, dissociation, defensive intellectualization, and hyperverbal self-awareness must be handled with specificity. The show does not glamorize dysregulation, but it does understand why it can feel productive, seductive, or socially rewarded.

Writing rule:
- We never ask the audience to laugh at suffering in the abstract; we ask them to recognize the behavior, the logic, and the cost.

### 7. Surreal Inserts Are Subjective Intensifiers
Surreal material is allowed when it expresses how a character experiences pressure, fantasy, authority, paranoia, or self-exoneration. It must not rewrite objective events.

Allowed functions:
- compressing manic association
- visualizing institutional dread
- staging fantasy versions of power or rescue
- puncturing self-mythology through contradiction

Not allowed:
- random sketch-comedy detours
- lore that changes the factual world
- dream logic used to avoid consequences

### 8. Consequences Accumulate
The world remembers. Messages are archived. Clients talk. Legal language matters. Emotional damage changes later scenes. Nothing fully resets just because the episode lands a laugh.

Seasonal consequence rules:
- every business win should leave residue
- every ethical shortcut should create later exposure
- every intimacy breach should alter future trust

### 9. Professionalism Is Performance, But Not Empty
The show should understand professional norms as partly theatrical without claiming they are meaningless. Contracts, decks, meetings, titles, and policy language matter because institutions act on them.

Writing rule:
- The joke is often that form can create legitimacy faster than substance, not that form has no power.

### 10. No Clean Villains
Clients, advisors, investors, and operators may be selfish, timid, predatory, or foolish, but they should not be written as simplistic antagonists. The main threat is a shared system of incentives that rewards speed, certainty, and plausible deniability.

## Boundaries for Realism
- No hacking fantasies that exceed the series' grounded tone.
- No sci-fi sentience plots around AI systems.
- No sudden legal outcomes that ignore process simply to accelerate drama.
- No visual screen grammar that makes digital work look generic or fake.
- No redemption beats that arrive without material cost.

## Dialogue Rules
- Characters can be hyperverbal, but every speech pattern must reveal status, defense, desire, or tactic.
- Jargon should sound native, not researched.
- Therapeutic language can be sincere and strategic in the same sentence.
- Memes and internet phrasing should be used as tools of self-positioning, not reference humor.

## Episode Reality Checks
Before locking any episode, verify:
- why the client would hire Cataclysm
- what concrete deliverable is at stake
- what digital artifact carries the turning point
- what psychological defense is operating in each lead
- what specific consequence survives the episode
