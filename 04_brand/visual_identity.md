## Brand Premise
The visual language for `WE OUT HERE` is premium desktop culture under stress. It should feel like expensive taste trying to hold together unstable people, sloppy ethics, and too many open windows.

The identity must hold four qualities at once:
- editorial confidence
- internet-native fluency
- startup rot
- psychological sharpness

It should never look like:
- generic AI product marketing
- a fake SaaS launch page
- broad cyberpunk
- a noisy meme collage without hierarchy
- prestige-TV minimalism so tasteful it loses behavioral specificity

## Core Visual Thesis
Design should feel as if the audience is seeing the clean outer shell and the corrupted working files at the same time.

Useful tension pairs:
- premium typography vs. ugly admin detail
- controlled layout vs. overflowing evidence
- black-field elegance vs. system-gray fatigue
- sharp portraiture vs. humiliating desktop realism

## Design Principles

### 1. Desktop Realism Is Material, Not Decoration
UI fragments, windows, cursors, timestamps, alerts, deck scaffolds, legal footers, and channel structures should behave like story artifacts, not generic texture.

### 2. The Show Is About People, Not Interfaces Alone
Faces, posture, fatigue, and interpersonal geometry matter. Interface treatment should intensify character, not replace it.

### 3. Clean Surfaces Must Carry Hidden Damage
Even the most premium layouts should include residue: annotation, compression, discoverability, contradictory metadata, revision traces, or notification pressure.

### 4. Avoid Generic AI Aesthetics
No floating blobs, rainbow gradients, glassmorphism for its own sake, abstract wireframes, or glowing-head silhouettes.

## Color System

### Primary Palette
- `Midnight Black` - near-black base for premium depth, not pure void
- `System White` - soft white with slight gray cast for docs and type
- `Compliance Gray` - cool neutral for frames, footers, dividers, metadata
- `Signal Red` - reserved for warnings, exposure, legal danger, and escalation
- `Terminal Blue` - reserved for selected UI emphasis and cold digital contrast

### Secondary Accent Palette
- `Mold Green` - limited use for dashboards, approvals, status chips, degraded success
- `Nicotine Amber` - notification heat, stress, stale office light
- `Bruise Plum` - surreal inserts, late-night emotional pressure, psychic distortion

### Palette Guidance
- Most campaign pieces should live in dark neutrals with one disciplined accent.
- Accent colors should feel operational, not playful.
- Red should mean consequence, not hype.
- Use color sparingly enough that a single alert state feels dangerous.

## Typography

### Primary Display Use
- Use a tense grotesk or editorial sans for title and major headlines.
- Tracking should feel intentional, sometimes slightly compressed.
- Weight should hold on posters and title cards without looking like blockbuster action branding.

### Secondary Functional Use
- Use a system-adjacent sans, mono, or utilitarian UI face for metadata, timestamps, legal language, labels, and desktop artifacts.
- Functional type should feel readable and real, not cosplay-tech.

### Typographic Behavior
- Headlines: sparse, controlled, declarative
- Metadata: dense, factual, low-drama
- Legal/admin microcopy: visible enough to imply consequence
- Taglines: sharp and brief, never over-written in the visual field

## Image System

### Photography And Stills
Favor images that show:
- overbright office or apartment light
- screen glow on faces
- controlled wardrobe with visible exhaustion
- triadic power imbalance inside one frame
- the comedy of people trying to look composed while obviously overextended

Avoid:
- broad laughing cast portraits
- anonymous hands on keyboards
- generic coder imagery
- fake holograms
- triumphant founder poses

### Framing Logic
- Close-ups should feel invasive, not glam-only.
- Wide shots should expose environment as workflow pressure.
- Use empty screen space, desk space, and room corners as stress fields.
- Let production design details reward inspection: cables, dead plants, stacked devices, legal pads, half-built decks, forgotten food.

## Layout System

### Composition Modes
Use three repeatable composition modes across campaign assets.

1. `Editorial Field`
Large title, strong still, disciplined metadata, minimal clutter.

2. `Desktop Evidence`
Windows, docs, timestamps, thread fragments, and annotations integrated into the layout.

3. `Psychic Overlay`
Still photography interrupted by surreal color, duplicated text, status warnings, or point-of-view distortion.

### Grid Guidance
- Prefer asymmetrical but controlled grids.
- Let modules feel placed, not templated.
- Use margins generously so clutter feels chosen.
- On mobile, collapse complexity but preserve tension through stacking, cropping, and metadata rhythm.

## Graphic Elements

### Approved Elements
- window frames
- cursor states
- timestamp blocks
- doc comments
- track-change marks
- alert bars
- status chips
- audit-like underlines
- crop marks and delivery notes in select trade-facing materials

### Restricted Elements
- glitch effects
- scan lines
- matrix rain
- neon code walls
- fake AI node networks
- rounded-product-UI cards that read as startup templates

## Motion Language

### Motion Feel
Motion should feel like a desktop action becoming emotionally loaded.

Good references in spirit:
- a cursor hesitating before send
- windows snapping into place with too much confidence
- a notification appearing at the wrong emotional moment
- a title resolving out of stacked drafts or comments

Avoid:
- trailers or teasers built on generic parallax motion
- logo glitch attacks
- excessive kinetic typography

### Motion Rules
- fast where pressure rises
- still where consequence lands
- short distortions instead of long abstract animation passages
- always preserve legibility of title and factual text

## In-World Brand Relationship
`Cataclysm Consulting` should have its own sub-brand texture inside the campaign, but it must sit beneath the main `WE OUT HERE` identity.

Cataclysm artifacts can be:
- more overbuilt
- more jargon-dense
- slightly more embarrassing
- more prone to fake authority signals

This contrast helps audiences understand that the series campaign is observing the company, not endorsing its taste.

## Wardrobe And Styling Cues For Marketing Shoots
- expensive basics degraded by overuse
- strong personal taste without perfect upkeep
- startup-black, nicotine neutrals, selective statement textures
- accessories that imply sleep loss, multitasking, and self-curation rather than luxury fantasy

## Practical Asset Requirements
- title wordmark set in light and dark versions
- social templates for 1:1, 4:5, 9:16, 16:9
- poster grid with legal/footer safe zones
- desktop overlay kit: windows, labels, comments, status chips, send states
- lower-third and title-card package for trailers
- LUT or grade guidance for key stills and motion capture

## Do / Don't

### Do
- make it feel authored
- make the digital surfaces specific
- keep the people emotionally legible
- preserve tension between polish and disrepair
- use emptiness strategically

### Don't
- make it look like an AI startup summit
- flatten the show into meme marketing
- over-explain the joke visually
- bury the title in interface clutter
- use too many accents, effects, or fonts

## Approval Questions
- Does this asset feel premium without becoming generic prestige minimalism?
- Does it feel internet-literate without collapsing into trend collage?
- Is startup rot present in the details?
- Are people still the center of the image system?
- Would this be mistaken for a SaaS launch page or AI conference ad? If yes, it fails.
