## Purpose

This glossary defines all project-specific terminology used across the WE OUT HERE production. It serves as a shared vocabulary for all agents, principals, crew, and collaborators. If a term appears in any project document and its meaning is not self-evident, it should be defined here.

**Usage:** When using a specialized term in any deliverable for the first time, reference this glossary. When reviewing a deliverable, check unfamiliar terms here before asking for clarification.

**Canon Reference:** Character and creative definitions in this glossary must be consistent with `00_admin/canon_lock.md` (WOH-ADMIN-000). This glossary elaborates but does not override canon.

## §2 — Show-Specific Creative Terms

| # | Term | Definition |
|---|---|---|
| 7 | **Desktop Realism** | A production style in which scenes are presented as if captured directly from a character's computer or phone screen. The audience sees what the character sees: chat windows, browser tabs, notifications, cursors, typing indicators, video calls. Distinguished from "screenlife" (see below) by its emphasis on realism — these are not stylized montages of screens but diegetically motivated recordings of actual digital interaction. |
| 8 | **Screenlife** | A broader genre/production term for narrative content presented through screens. Films like SEARCHING (2018), UNFRIENDED (2014), and PROFILE (2018) are screenlife. WE OUT HERE uses screenlife as one of three production modes. In this project, "screenlife" and "desktop realism" are often used interchangeably, though "desktop realism" emphasizes the grounded, un-stylized quality of the show's approach. |
| 9 | **Surreal Insert** | A sequence that departs from realist staging into stylized, dreamlike, or metaphorical visual territory. Surreal inserts externalize a character's psychological state — making visible what is normally invisible (anxiety, dissociation, grandiosity, paranoia). They are brief (typically 15–90 seconds), psychologically motivated, and must earn their departure from realism. Approximately 10% of total screen time. |
| 10 | **Live Action** | Traditional single-camera film/television coverage — actors in physical spaces, shot with standard cinematographic grammar. Approximately 50–60% of total screen time. The "default mode" of the show when characters are interacting in physical space rather than through screens. |
| 11 | **Hybrid Format** | The defining production approach of WE OUT HERE: the interweaving of desktop realism, live action, and surreal inserts within and across episodes. The transitions between modes are load-bearing — they signal shifts in register, intimacy, and psychological reality. |
| 12 | **Tech Psychocomedy** | The show's genre descriptor (used alongside "dark sitcom"). Comedy that emerges from the intersection of technology culture and psychological defense mechanisms. Not tech satire alone (which would be observational) and not psychological drama alone (which would be earnest). The comedy IS the psychology. |
| 13 | **Dark Sitcom** | A situation comedy that does not reset to stability at the end of each episode. Problems compound. Wins create new problems. The instability is cumulative across the season. Distinguished from "dark comedy" (a film genre) by its episodic structure and character-driven humor. |
| 14 | **One-Way Ratchet** | The structural principle governing the season arc: instability accumulates and does not reverse. Each episode's resolution creates conditions for the next episode's crisis. There is no "bounce back" episode. Borrowed from mechanical engineering — a ratchet permits motion in only one direction. |
| 15 | **Failed Holding Environment** | The show's central metaphor (from psychoanalytic theory). The trio's friendship and their group chat are supposed to function as a holding environment — a relational container for anxiety and vulnerability. They fail at this. The failure is what makes the show both funny and painful. See §4 for the full psychoanalytic definition. |
| 16 | **Reaction GIF Logic** | bl0ng's primary communication mode: responding to situations with ironic detachment mediated through memes, GIFs, reaction images, and sardonic one-liners rather than direct emotional engagement. A defense mechanism rendered in internet-native form. |
| 17 | **Chat Dynamics** | The visual and narrative grammar of how conversations unfold in the fictional chat platform: typing indicators, message edits, deletions, emoji reactions, @mentions, thread branching, read receipts. These micro-interactions carry dramatic weight. A typing indicator that starts and stops IS a dramatic beat. |
| 18 | **The Group Chat** | The primary dramatic space of the show — `Backchannel`, the fictional group-chat platform where twinkle.puss, bl0ng, and ariane.emory conduct their business, manage their crises, and fail to manage their relationships. The group chat is not just a setting; it is a character. It contains what the characters cannot say to each other's faces. |
| 19 | **Exposure to Consequences** | The final beat of the season arc (S01E06 "Let Him Cook()"). Not redemption, not punishment, not catharsis — but the characters being forced to see and reckon with what their actions have produced. The show does not moralize; it reveals. |
| 20 | **The Manic Defense** | Both an episode title (S01E03) and a psychoanalytic concept deployed in the show. See §4 for the clinical definition. In the show's context: the strategy of escalating energy, productivity, and grandiosity to avoid confronting depression, loss, or failure. twinkle.puss's default operating mode. |

## §3 — Production & Technical Terms

| # | Term | Definition |
|---|---|---|
| 21 | **Single-Camera (Single-Cam)** | A production method using one camera (or one camera setup at a time) per shot, typical of prestige comedy and drama. Allows more cinematic composition and lighting than multi-cam. WE OUT HERE is single-cam for all live-action sequences. |
| 22 | **Screen Capture Pipeline** | The technical workflow for recording screenlife content: building the functional chat platform UI, populating it with scripted messages, and recording the screen at production resolution. Distinguished from "filming a screen with a camera" — the capture IS the camera. |
| 23 | **UI/UX Design (Production Context)** | In WE OUT HERE, UI/UX design is not a post-production task — it is a production design task. The fictional chat platform must be designed, built, and functional before screenlife scenes can be captured. The UI designer is a creative collaborator, not a vendor. |
| 24 | **Standing Set** | A permanent or semi-permanent set built or dressed on a stage/warehouse that remains available for the duration of principal photography. WE OUT HERE assumes 2–3 standing sets for recurring locations (e.g., the characters' primary workspace, a secondary living space). |
| 25 | **Practical Location** | A real-world location (not a purpose-built set) used for filming. WE OUT HERE assumes 3–5 practicals across the season (e.g., coffee shops, co-working spaces, client offices). |
| 26 | **Block Shooting** | A scheduling method where scenes from multiple episodes are shot together based on location or actor availability rather than episode order. Maximizes efficiency by reducing company moves. |
| 27 | **Cover Set** | An interior backup location (usually a standing set) that can be used when exterior shooting is canceled due to weather or other disruption. A schedule insurance policy. |
| 28 | **Day Player** | A crew member or actor hired for a single day or a small number of days, rather than for the full production run. Common for specialized roles or guest cast. |
| 29 | **Company Move** | The relocation of the entire production crew from one shooting location to another within the same shooting day. Company moves cost time and money; minimizing them is a scheduling priority. |
| 30 | **Principal Photography** | The main period of film/TV production when scenes are shot with the primary cast and crew. Distinguished from pre-production (planning) and post-production (editing). |
| 31 | **Proof-of-Concept (POC)** | A short piece of produced material (a scene, a sizzle reel, or a pilot-length sample) used to demonstrate the show's tone, visual style, and hybrid format to potential investors, cast, or platform buyers. |
| 32 | **Sizzle Reel** | A short, edited presentation (typically 2–5 minutes) combining footage, concept art, mood references, and text to convey the feel and pitch of a project. Used in packaging and financing. Not a trailer (trailers are cut from finished footage). |
| 33 | **Table Read** | A seated reading of a full script by actors (or stand-ins), attended by writers and key creatives. Used to test pacing, joke timing, and character voice before production. A critical quality gate. |
| 34 | **Call Sheet** | A daily production document listing the schedule, scenes, cast calls, crew calls, locations, weather forecast, and contact information for each shooting day. The single most important logistics document during production. |
| 35 | **AD (Assistant Director)** | The crew member responsible for managing the set schedule, calling shots, maintaining discipline, and ensuring the day's work is completed. The 1st AD is the primary schedule enforcer on set. |

## §4 — Psychoanalytic & Psychological Terms

These terms are drawn from psychoanalytic theory and are referenced throughout the show's creative documents. The show uses these concepts to structure character behavior and dramatic logic — they are load-bearing, not decorative.

| # | Term | Definition |
|---|---|---|
| 36 | **Defense Mechanism** | An unconscious psychological strategy used to protect the self from anxiety, conflict, or intolerable feelings. In WE OUT HERE, each character's primary defense mechanism defines their comedic and dramatic behavior. Comedy emerges FROM the defenses, not despite them. |
| 37 | **Holding Environment** | (D.W. Winnicott) A relational space — originally the parent-infant relationship — that provides safety, containment, and predictability, allowing the held person to experience and process difficult feelings without being overwhelmed. In WE OUT HERE, the group chat and the trio's friendship are supposed to function as holding environments. They fail. The failure is the show. |
| 38 | **Containment** | (Wilfred Bion) The capacity to receive, hold, and metabolize another person's projected emotional states — particularly anxiety, rage, and despair — without retaliating, collapsing, or passing the distress back unprocessed. S01E04 "Contain Me Daddy" is named for the failure of this function. |
| 39 | **Superego** | (Sigmund Freud) The moral component of the psyche — the internalized voice of rules, conscience, prohibition, and judgment. bl0ng IS the superego of the trio, but a failed one: he sees what's wrong but cannot bring himself to intervene directly. S01E05 "The Superego Collapse" is named for the moment this function gives out entirely. |
| 40 | **The Manic Defense** | (Melanie Klein) A psychological defense against depressive anxiety characterized by omnipotence, denial of psychic reality, contempt, and triumph. In non-clinical terms: going into overdrive (energy, productivity, grandiosity) to avoid feeling loss, smallness, or failure. twinkle.puss's baseline state. S01E03 is titled "The Manic Defense." |
| 41 | **Projection** | A defense mechanism in which intolerable feelings or qualities are attributed to someone else. "I'm not anxious — YOU'RE anxious." In the show, the trio constantly projects onto each other, onto clients, and onto the group chat itself. |
| 42 | **Dissociation** | A psychological process of disconnection from one's thoughts, feelings, surroundings, or sense of identity. ariane.emory's primary mode: she processes the world from a cognitive remove, treating emotional reality as an intellectual problem. The surreal inserts often externalize dissociative states. |
| 43 | **Splitting** | A defense mechanism in which people or situations are perceived as all-good or all-bad, with no integration of ambivalence. The trio splits their clients, their company, and each other. A client is either a savior or an enemy; a deal is either a breakthrough or a catastrophe. |
| 44 | **Ironic Detachment** | Not a formal psychoanalytic term, but a defense pattern central to bl0ng: using irony, humor, and performative indifference to maintain emotional distance from situations that are actually threatening or painful. bl0ng's ironic detachment is his version of the manic defense — it's just quieter. |
| 45 | **Transference** | (Freud) The redirection of feelings and desires originally associated with one person (typically a parent or early caregiver) onto another person. In the show, the trio's relationships with their clients are loaded with transferential dynamics — clients become parental figures, persecutors, or dependents. |
| 46 | **Countertransference** | The therapist's (or in this case, the consultant's) emotional response to the client's transference. When twinkle.puss starts acting out because a client reminds him of an authority figure who dismissed him, that's countertransference without self-awareness. |
| 47 | **Acting Out** | Expressing unconscious conflicts through behavior rather than words or reflection. twinkle.puss's primary mode: instead of talking about anxiety, he sends the email, signs the contract, launches the product, makes the deal. Every impulsive action is an acted-out feeling. |
| 48 | **Psychic Retreat** | (John Steiner) A mental state to which one withdraws when the pain of engagement with reality becomes intolerable. ariane.emory's meta-observer position is a psychic retreat — she retreats into analysis, aesthetics, and systems-thinking to avoid the vulnerability of genuine connection. |
| 49 | **Reparation** | (Klein) The desire to repair damage done to a loved object (person, relationship, group). Reparation is what the trio CANNOT quite do. They know they're hurting each other and the company. They try, occasionally, to fix things. They fail. The reparation attempts generate comedy because they are always slightly wrong. |

## §5 — Business, Legal & Financial Terms

| # | Term | Definition |
|---|---|---|
| 50 | **Out Here Productions LLC** | The provisional name for the production entity that will hold all IP, contracts, and financial accounts for WE OUT HERE. A Delaware LLC. Entity has NOT been formed yet as of this document's creation date. Soft lock — name may change. |
| 51 | **LLC (Limited Liability Company)** | A U.S. business structure that provides pass-through taxation and limited personal liability for its members. The standard entity type for indie film/TV production. |
| 52 | **Operating Agreement** | The internal governance document of an LLC, defining member rights, profit distribution, decision-making authority, and dissolution procedures. Distinct from a partnership agreement. The constitutional document of the production entity. |
| 53 | **Chain of Title** | The documented history of IP ownership from creation to current holder. A clean chain of title — showing that Out Here Productions LLC owns all rights to WE OUT HERE — is required for E&O insurance and platform acquisition. Every contributor must sign IP assignment or work-for-hire agreements. |
| 54 | **E&O Insurance (Errors and Omissions)** | A liability insurance policy that protects the production against claims of defamation, copyright infringement, invasion of privacy, and other IP-related liabilities. Required by virtually every distributor and platform. E&O coverage typically requires a legal opinion letter confirming the chain of title and script clearance. |
| 55 | **SAG-AFTRA New Media Agreement** | A contract framework from the Screen Actors Guild-American Federation of Television and Radio Artists designed for content produced for new media/streaming platforms with budgets below certain thresholds. Offers reduced rates and modified terms compared to standard television agreements. The assumed union framework for WE OUT HERE cast. |
| 56 | **Work-for-Hire** | A legal doctrine (U.S. copyright law) under which the employer, not the creator, is considered the author and owner of a work created within the scope of employment or under a qualifying agreement. Essential for ensuring the production entity owns all contributions. |
| 57 | **IP Assignment** | A legal document in which a contributor transfers ownership of their intellectual property (ideas, writing, designs) to the production entity. Used for contributions that don't qualify as work-for-hire. |
| 58 | **Above-the-Line (ATL)** | Budget category encompassing creative leadership: writers, director(s), producers, and principal cast. Costs are typically fixed and negotiated individually. In WE OUT HERE, approximately 25–35% of total budget. |
| 59 | **Below-the-Line (BTL)** | Budget category encompassing all crew, equipment, locations, materials, and day-to-day production costs. Managed as a line-item budget with per-day and per-department rates. In WE OUT HERE, approximately 40–50% of total budget. |
| 60 | **Tax Incentive** | A state or national program that provides rebates, credits, or grants to film/TV productions that shoot in a specific jurisdiction and meet qualifying criteria (spending thresholds, local hiring). Can reduce effective production cost by 15–35%. |
| 61 | **Bridge Financing** | Short-term funding used to cover cash flow gaps between committed investor payments and actual production expenses. Common in indie production where investor tranches arrive on different schedules than vendor invoices. |
| 62 | **Platform Acquisition** | The sale or licensing of a completed series to a streaming platform (e.g., Hulu, Peacock, Amazon). Terms typically include a license fee, territory, and term (number of years). Distinguished from a co-production (where the platform is involved from development). |
| 63 | **TVOD / SVOD / AVOD / FAST** | Distribution models. TVOD = transactional video on demand (pay per view, e.g., iTunes rental). SVOD = subscription video on demand (e.g., Netflix, Hulu). AVOD = advertising-supported video on demand (e.g., Tubi, Pluto TV). FAST = free ad-supported streaming television (linear channels on platforms like Roku, Samsung TV+). |
| 64 | **Deliverables (Distribution Context)** | The technical package required by a distributor or platform: master files in specified formats (ProRes, H.264), closed captions, subtitles, key art, metadata, trailer, EPK materials. Spec requirements vary by platform. |
| 65 | **NDA (Non-Disclosure Agreement)** | A contract prohibiting the recipient from sharing confidential information. All WE OUT HERE personnel sign NDAs covering scripts, story materials, cast information, and business terms. |

## §6 — Platform & Technology Terms (Show's World)

These terms describe the technology ecosystem that exists within the fictional world of WE OUT HERE. They are relevant to writing, production design, and the screenlife pipeline.

| # | Term | Definition |
|---|---|---|
| 66 | **The Platform** | `Backchannel`, the fictional group-chat application used by the trio. It is never called "Discord" in the show (per canon_lock.md §1.3). It is legally and narratively distinct from any real-world platform. It is the primary dramatic space of the show — more scenes take place here than in any physical location. |
| 67 | **Channel** | A topic-organized conversation space within the platform (equivalent to a Discord channel or Slack channel). The trio's platform likely has channels for: general, clients, invoicing, memes, existential-dread, etc. Channel names are a worldbuilding detail defined in the world bible. |
| 68 | **Thread** | A nested conversation branching off from a message in a channel. Threads allow sidebar discussions without cluttering the main channel. Dramatically useful for showing private conversations happening in parallel to public ones. |
| 69 | **Typing Indicator** | The animated "..." or "[user] is typing" signal that appears when someone is composing a message. In WE OUT HERE, a typing indicator that starts, stops, starts again, and then resolves into a single-word message is a complete dramatic beat. |
| 70 | **Read Receipt** | A signal (checkmark, timestamp, or "seen by") indicating that a message has been viewed by its recipient. Dramatically loaded: a message that's been read but not replied to is a silence that speaks. |
| 71 | **@ Mention** | Tagging a specific user in a message to direct their attention. In the show, an @mention is an act of accountability, confrontation, or passive-aggression depending on context. |
| 72 | **Emoji React** | A response to a message consisting of a single emoji rather than a text reply. In bl0ng's communication style, an emoji react IS the reply — it says everything he refuses to say in words. A skull emoji (💀) on a message about a client contract is bl0ng's entire review. |
| 73 | **DM (Direct Message)** | A private, one-on-one message between two users outside of any shared channel. DMs are where the trio says what they won't say in the group — alliances, complaints, vulnerable admissions. DMs are secrets the audience is allowed to overhear. |
| 74 | **Bot** | An automated agent within the platform that performs functions (scheduling, reminders, moderation, custom commands). The trio's platform may include a bot that becomes a comedic character in its own right — e.g., a malfunctioning meeting-scheduler bot that keeps creating events no one attends. |
| 75 | **Server** | The platform's term for a self-contained community within `Backchannel` (equivalent to a Discord server). Cataclysm Consulting's server is the company's entire digital infrastructure — its office, its culture, its institutional memory, and eventually its crime scene. |
| 76 | **AI Consultancy** | The fictional business model of Cataclysm Consulting: offering AI strategy, implementation, and advisory services to clients. The trio may or may not actually understand AI — they understand how to talk about it convincingly enough to get contracts. The gap between their pitch and their competence is a core comedic engine. |
| 77 | **Prompt Engineering** | The practice of designing inputs (prompts) for large language models to produce desired outputs. In the show's world, this is likely one of the services Cataclysm Consulting offers — or claims to offer. The term itself has comedic potential: it sounds like engineering but is more like persuasion. |

## §7 — Project Operations & Document Terms

| # | Term | Definition |
|---|---|---|

| 92 | **Document ID** | A unique identifier assigned to every project deliverable, following the format `WOH-[DIR]-[NUM]`. Examples: WOH-ADMIN-000 (canon_lock.md), WOH-ADMIN-001 (studio_manifest.md). Used for cross-referencing and version tracking. |
