# 13_post/music_strategy.md

## Purpose

Define the music strategy for **WE OUT HERE**, covering score philosophy, source-music policy, licensing boundaries, workflow with editorial and sound, and deliverable implications for festival, streamer, and digital release paths. This document is meant to let producers, composers, music supervisors, and post vendors plan realistically against budget and rights constraints.

## Strategic Position

WE OUT HERE should not depend on expensive recognizable songs to function. The music plan should be built around authored original score, selective commissioned material, and controlled library usage, with licensing kept simple enough that festival play, streamer acquisition, and direct digital release remain viable.

## Music Goals

- Support the show's psychological-comic tension without flattening scenes into obvious satire
- Give the hybrid format a sonic identity that can move between embodied live action, digital tension, and surreal insert logic
- Avoid needle-drop dependence that would create clearance risk, budget strain, or replacement problems later
- Build a reusable score language across six episodes so the season feels coherent

## Score Philosophy

The score should feel internet-literate and emotionally precise, but not like parody startup music or default prestige-drama underscore.

- Favor motifs that can mutate between low-key dread, manic momentum, and detached irony
- Let some cues begin almost diegetically, as if emerging from device logic, browser rhythm, or typing cadence
- Avoid over-signaling jokes; the comedy should often arrive from contrast and timing rather than musical punchlines
- Reserve bigger musical moves for turning points, collapse sequences, and surreal escalations

## Recommended Music Palette

- restrained electronic textures
- brittle synthetic rhythm beds
- anxious percussive pulse
- degraded or glitched tonal elements used sparingly
- intimate, minimal harmonic writing for moments of real exposure

The exact palette can evolve with the composer, but it should remain modular enough to support recuts and alternate lengths during post.

## Source Music Policy

### Preferred Sources

- original score
- commissioned tracks with clear buyout terms or broad production rights
- pre-cleared library music from reputable vendors

### Avoid By Default

- recognizable commercial masters that trigger expensive festival-to-streamer relicensing
- temp tracks the show cannot afford to keep
- ironic pop drops that do the joke for the scene

### If A Needle Drop Is Proposed

It must pass all of the following tests:

1. creative necessity is real, not just preference
2. cost fits both festival and downstream exploitation windows
3. publishing and master rights are both realistically clearable
4. fallback plan exists if rights fail late

## Composer Workflow

Recommended process:

1. composer brought in by fine-cut stage on Episode 1
2. create a short tonal suite before full episodic cueing
3. establish recurring motif families for mania, drift, containment failure, and consequence exposure
4. score to locked scenes, not unstable assemblies, unless an early creative test is specifically requested
5. deliver alternates and stemmed cues where editorial flexibility is needed

## Temp Music Policy

- Temp music may be used to indicate energy and shape, but should not become an unbudgeted promise
- Editors should flag any temp cue that materially influences scene timing or approval discussion
- The post supervisor should maintain a temp-license risk list so no one assumes keep rights exist

## Cue Types For WE OUT HERE

### Character / State Cues

- manic propulsion cues for twinkle.puss-driven escalation
- suppressed-dread or ironic restraint cues for bl0ng-adjacent scenes
- dissociative observational textures for ariane.emory-linked moments

### Structural Cues

- cold open launch energy
- montage / process rhythm
- collapse or fallout cueing
- episode-end consequence drift

### Hybrid Cues

- cues that interlock with message timing, typing rhythm, or notification cascades
- transitions from objective space into surreal insert subjectivity

## Coordination With Sound Post

- UI sounds and score must be developed in parallel on key scenes, not in isolation
- If a scene's tension comes from notification behavior, score should support the beat architecture rather than drown it
- The composer and sound designer should share stems or preview passes on at least one representative chat-heavy scene early in the season

## Rights And Clearance Strategy

- All music agreements should contemplate festival exhibition, streamer licensing, TVOD/SVOD/AVOD use, trailers, and promo excerpts where possible
- Composer agreements should define ownership, backend, soundtrack rights, revision limits, and deliverable obligations clearly
- Library tracks must be documented with license term, territories, media, and replacement risk
- Cue sheets should be prepared cleanly at final delivery, not reconstructed later under deadline

## Deliverable Requirements For Music

Per episode, music department should plan to deliver:

- final stereo mix cues
- cue stems where needed
- cue list with in/out timecodes
- composer metadata and rights notes
- final cue sheet information for reporting

For any source cue, archive:

- license agreement
- proof of payment
- term / territory summary
- replacement contact path if later versioning is required

## Festival / Streamer / Digital Implications

### Festival

- Festival rights can sometimes be narrower, but do not build the whole season around festival-only music if streamer sales remain a core target

### Streamer

- Buyers prefer low-clearance-risk packages; broad rights and clean documentation help acquisition diligence

### Digital Release

- Direct release often exposes projects to long-tail rights problems if terms are too narrow; perpetual or long-term broad rights are preferable where affordable

## Budget Discipline

- Put most resources into original score and one strong show language rather than scattered premium licenses
- Hold a reserve for one or two strategic source moments only if justified creatively
- Treat music editorial and licensing admin as real labor, not invisible overhead

## Common Failure Modes To Avoid

- temp-love lock causing expensive or impossible replacement
- over-scoring dialogue scenes that are funnier dry
- using music to announce emotion that performance already carries
- unclear rights paperwork delaying delivery or buyer diligence
