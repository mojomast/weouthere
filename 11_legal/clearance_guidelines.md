# 11_legal/clearance_guidelines.md

## Purpose

Set practical day-to-day clearance rules for WE OUT HERE so the team can make creative and production decisions that reduce legal friction before those issues become expensive. This is an internal guideline, not final legal advice.

## Owner/Subagent
legal_agent

## Status
Draft v1 - internal guidance. Working draft only. Clearance judgments can be fact-specific and should be escalated to counsel when material, ambiguous, or release-critical.

## Dependencies

- Current scripts and breakdowns
- Production design plans
- Graphic and UI design files
- Music plan and edit references
- Marketing asset plan
- Counsel input on flagged items

## Plain-Language Rule

If the team did not create it, assume it needs review before it goes on screen, in audio, or in marketing.

## Core Clearance Categories

### 1. Brands, logos, and trade dress

- Avoid featuring recognizable third-party marks unless the use is intentional, necessary, and reviewed.
- Cover or replace visible brand logos where practical.
- Be careful not to create fake sponsorship or endorsement implications.
- Distinctive product packaging, app layouts, and branded visual systems can create issues even when a logo is not visible.

### 2. Fictional platform materials

This category deserves special attention for WE OUT HERE.

- Use a fictional platform name and original interface design.
- Do not rely on literal Discord logos, names, mascots, color treatments, or near-matching server/chat UI.
- Avoid duplicated language that reads as platform-native branding rather than a generalized concept.
- Review every chat screen, app screen, and notification asset before shooting and again before lock.

### 3. Artwork, photos, posters, and set dressing

- Assume visible artwork and photos need rights unless clearly original, licensed, or public domain.
- Do not use internet images casually in bedrooms, offices, or wall collages.
- Keep a source list for all set dressing with ownership or license status.

### 4. Music and audio

- Do not cut with temp music unless everyone knows it is temp.
- No pre-existing music should remain in picture lock, trailer cuts, or social cuts without clearance confirmation.
- Background diegetic music still matters.

### 5. Dialogue and script references

- Casual mentions of real brands may be lower risk than visual imitation, but they still need judgment.
- Avoid unnecessary use of real slogans, song lyrics, or lengthy quotations.
- If a joke only works by naming a real company or person, flag it for review instead of assuming it is fine.

### 6. Real people and identifying details

- Avoid using real names, handles, phone numbers, addresses, and identifiable biographical details unless cleared or legally vetted.
- Change combinations of details if a fictional character could be traced to a real person.

### 7. Online content and screenshots

- Screenshots from apps, websites, social platforms, streams, or chats should be treated as clearance-sensitive.
- Recreate fictional screens from scratch where possible.
- Do not assume publicly available means free to use.

## Department Rules

### Writing

- Flag lyrics, quotes, real posts, and real-platform references in the script.
- Prefer fictional names where story value does not depend on a real brand.

### Production design

- Maintain an asset log for set dressing, props, wardrobe marks, and graphic inserts.
- Clear or replace visible risky items before shoot day.

### Camera and directing

- Avoid accidental background captures of brands, art, or bystanders where possible.
- Call out screens, posters, and wardrobe logos during tech scouts and on set.

### Editorial and post

- Review every frame for logos, posters, interface screens, and stock assets.
- Keep alternate replacement options ready for risky inserts.
- Re-check trailer and social edits; they often reintroduce risky material.

### Marketing

- Do not use unlicensed memes, reposted fan art, or third-party clips in promo.
- Make sure key art, typography, and motion graphics are owned or licensed.

## Escalation Triggers

Escalate to legal review if:

- a real brand is featured prominently
- the fictional platform resembles Discord or another real service closely enough to invite confusion
- a scene relies on a real person's story or identifying facts
- a use is being justified mainly by "everyone does this"
- the team wants to rely on fair use, parody, or satire rather than replacing the material

## Practical Notes

- Replacing an asset before shooting is cheaper than painting it out later.
- Social and promo teams can create new clearance problems even if the show itself is clean.
- Clearance is easiest when it is assigned to named people in each department.

## Attorney Review Flags

- Review of all high-visibility brand, title, and interface depictions.
- Review of any real-person inspired scenes or references.
- Review of any intended fair use, parody, or documentary-style argument.
- Review of final title treatment and key art before public launch.

## Working Legal Position

These guidelines are internal risk-reduction practices only and not final jurisdiction-specific legal advice. Final clearance calls depend on context, prominence, territory, and actual use.
