# 11_legal/rights_and_chain_of_title.md

## Purpose

Create a practical rights and chain-of-title playbook for WE OUT HERE so the team can prove ownership, document permissions, and avoid avoidable clearance gaps before financing, production, insurance, festival submissions, or distribution. This is an internal planning document and not final legal advice.

## Project Assumption

WE OUT HERE is an original scripted sitcom IP. The project should be documented as a wholly original work unless and until counsel confirms that any third-party underlying material has been fully licensed.

Important creative clearance assumption:

- The show may depict a fictional online chat or community platform.
- The production should not rely on literal Discord branding, logos, trade dress, UI duplication, or language that suggests sponsorship or official affiliation.
- If the scripts currently contain literal brand references, those references should be reviewed scene by scene for replacement, clearance, or risk tolerance.

## Plain-Language Goal

If a buyer, insurer, or lawyer asks, "Who owns this show and why can you exploit it?" the answer should be simple, written, signed, and easy to follow.

In practice, that means the file trail should show:

1. Who created the original sitcom concept.
2. Who owns it now.
3. What each later contributor made.
4. That each contributor signed an agreement transferring or licensing the needed rights.
5. That all third-party materials were either avoided, replaced, or properly licensed.

## Required Chain-Of-Title Story

The cleanest working story for WE OUT HERE is:

1. The original creator or creators created the series concept, characters, pilot, and related development materials.
2. Those rights were assigned in writing to the project rights-holding entity, or were created under a valid work-made-for-hire and assignment agreement.
3. Every later contributor signed an agreement before or at the time of contribution, with present-tense assignment language transferring all right, title, and interest needed for production and exploitation.
4. All third-party elements used in scripts, production design, marketing, and final episodes were either original, licensed, in the public domain, or approved by counsel under a defensible legal theory.

## Minimum Rights File Checklist

These are the core documents the production should expect to assemble.

### Underlying IP

- Creator assignment to the rights-holding entity
- Split sheet if there is more than one creator
- Dated drafts showing project evolution
- Proof that the concept is original and not adapted from unlicensed source material

### Writing And Development

- Writer agreement for every writer, room participant, consultant, or script polisher
- Written confirmation of credit treatment and payment terms
- Assignment of revisions, notes, outlines, jokes, punch-ups, and pitch materials

### Production Contributions

- Contractor agreement for directors, editors, designers, animators, and key creative vendors
- Composer, music producer, and soundtrack agreements if applicable
- Graphic design and key art assignments
- Website, social, promo, and trailer asset assignments

### Cast And Likeness

- Talent release or performer agreement for every on-camera performer
- Parent or guardian documentation if any minor is ever involved
- Releases for voiceover or ADR performers

### Locations, Art, Props, And Footage

- Location agreement where needed
- Artwork releases or licenses for visible art
- Product, logo, and signage review log
- Stock footage, archive, still photo, and clip licenses where used

### Music And Audio

- Composer agreement
- Master use and sync licenses for pre-existing music
- Cue sheet process
- Documentation showing no temp music was left in by mistake

### Business Records

- Fully signed PDFs in one controlled repository
- Version control so final signed forms can be identified quickly
- Payment records tied to the applicable agreements
- Contact sheet for everyone who signed or granted rights

## Rights Matrix

| Asset / contribution | Default risk | Required document | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Series concept and characters | Ownership dispute | Creator assignment | Must cover sequels, spin-offs, remakes, and promo use |
| Pilot and episode scripts | Co-author claim | Writer agreement | Include work-made-for-hire plus assignment backup |
| Punch-up jokes and room notes | Informal contribution claim | Short-form writer or consulting agreement | Do not let unpaid informal writing accumulate |
| Directing and editing choices | Authorship or approval claim | Contractor agreement | Include waiver of injunctive relief where enforceable |
| Graphic chat screens and fictional UI | Trademark/trade dress risk | Contractor agreement plus clearance review | Avoid literal Discord look and feel |
| Cast performance and likeness | Use of name/image claim | Talent release | Include marketing, clips, trailers, and behind-the-scenes uses |
| Music | Copyright strike or delivery failure | Composer or music license agreements | One uncleared track can stop release |
| Photos, posters, wall art, apparel logos | Clearance failure | License, release, or replacement memo | Review frame by frame for visible issues |

## Chain-Of-Title Procedure

### Before meaningful creative work starts

1. Identify every person contributing protectable expression.
2. Get signatures before work starts where possible.
3. Use one legal entity consistently as the contracting party.

### During development

1. Save dated drafts and meeting notes.
2. Track who wrote what if multiple writers are involved.
3. Do not circulate external pitch materials without ownership language in place.

### Before production spend escalates

1. Confirm all key creatives are under signed agreements.
2. Review script references to brands, apps, music, clips, and real people.
3. Build a clearance list from the script breakdown.

### Before release, submissions, or insurer review

1. Match every visible and audible third-party element to a release, license, or removal decision.
2. Confirm final versions of all agreements are signed.
3. Prepare a delivery folder that an E&O underwriter or distributor can actually follow.

## Common Failure Modes

These are the problems that most often create chain-of-title pain on indie projects:

- A friend "helped with jokes" but never signed anything.
- A contractor invoice exists, but no IP assignment exists.
- The show bible was co-created, but the split was never documented.
- A pitch deck used borrowed images that later leaked into official promo materials.
- The fictional platform design got too close to a real platform's protected branding or trade dress.
- A temp song or background poster made it into the locked cut.
- The company name changed across documents, making the ownership trail muddy.

## Practical Notes

- Payment alone does not reliably prove IP ownership.
- "We talked about it" is not chain of title.
- Screenshots, shared docs, and text messages can help explain history, but they are not substitutes for signed agreements.
- If someone contributed before paperwork existed, get a retroactive confirmation and assignment reviewed by counsel as soon as possible.
- Keep fully signed copies, not just signature pages.

## Attorney Review Flags

Counsel should review these issues directly before the project relies on this package:

- Whether all creator and contributor agreements use present-tense assignment language strong enough for the relevant jurisdictions.
- Whether work-made-for-hire treatment is legally effective for each contributor category.
- Whether any contributor may assert copyright co-authorship, moral rights, approval rights, credit rights, or termination rights.
- Whether any scripts, jokes, scenes, or platform elements are too close to identifiable third-party works, brands, or interfaces.
- Whether the title WE OUT HERE creates any title or trademark conflict in entertainment categories.
- Whether any real-person inspiration creates right-of-publicity, privacy, or defamation exposure.
- Whether the planned distribution path requires additional delivery documents or releases.

## Working Legal Position

This document is a practical internal checklist, not jurisdiction-specific legal advice. Final chain-of-title sufficiency depends on the actual facts, the signed agreements, the territories of exploitation, and distributor and insurer requirements. Qualified entertainment counsel should review the final rights file before release.
