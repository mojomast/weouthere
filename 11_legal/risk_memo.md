## Executive View

WE OUT HERE is legally manageable as an original scripted sitcom, but the project has several predictable indie risks that should be handled early rather than patched late.

The biggest likely pressure points are:

1. chain of title gaps from informal collaboration
2. fictional platform depiction drifting too close to real-world Discord branding or trade dress
3. uncleared third-party elements in scripts, production design, edit, or marketing
4. character or dialogue choices that create defamation, privacy, or publicity risk
5. contract and worker-classification shortcuts that become delivery or payment disputes

## Key Risks In Plain Language

### 1. Informal collaboration risk

Indie projects often accumulate real authorship from friends, consultants, punch-up help, unpaid readers, designers, and early editors before paperwork exists. That is the fastest way to create chain-of-title ambiguity.

What to do now:

- identify everyone who contributed protectable material
- sign retroactive confirmations where necessary
- stop taking material contributions without paperwork

### 2. Fictional platform depiction risk

This is the most project-specific issue.

The show can absolutely depict a fictional online community platform. The problem starts when the fictional product looks, sounds, or behaves so much like Discord that the audience reads it as the real brand or an endorsed stand-in.

Risk triggers include:

- copied iconography or color systems
- duplicated UI layout or terminology
- realistic screenshots or overlays too close to the real service
- marketing copy implying the show is about or with the actual platform

What to do now:

- pick a fictional platform name
- redesign visual systems and interaction language
- review any real-brand references for necessity

### 3. Third-party asset risk

Posters on walls, shirt logos, phone screens, temporary music, website screenshots, and stock assets often slip in because they seem small. Those small things are exactly what can delay insurer approval or trigger takedown demands.

What to do now:

- create a clearance log from the script breakdown onward
- require approvals for every non-original asset
- do a frame-by-frame review before lock

### 4. Character and story sensitivity risk

Even fictional comedy can create exposure if viewers can identify a real person behind a character, especially in a niche scene or community.

What to do now:

- avoid one-to-one character lifts
- change combinations of traits, history, and role if needed
- escalate any character based on a real anecdote for review

### 5. Contracting and labor risk

Poorly matched paperwork can create later disputes over payment, ownership, credit, or worker status.

What to do now:

- use the right form for writers, contractors, and talent
- do not rely on invoices or DMs as the full agreement
- check whether any role should be on payroll rather than treated as a contractor

## Release-Stage Risks

Risks usually increase at three moments:

1. when money is being raised
2. when principal photography starts
3. when a cut is shown outside the core team

At those moments, the team should expect harder questions about:

- who owns the IP
- whether visible and audible assets are cleared
- whether there is insurance-ready paperwork
- whether anyone can claim the production used their likeness, story, or work without permission

## Practical Red Flags

Escalate immediately if any of these are true:

- a contributor says "we can paper it later"
- a script page includes copied lyrics, lengthy quotes, or screenshot references that may end up on screen
- design work includes a near-clone of Discord or another real platform
- anyone says a character is "basically" a specific real person
- marketing wants to use memes, reposts, or third-party clips because "everyone does it"

## Attorney Review Flags

- Review of scripts and designs for trademark, trade dress, defamation, privacy, and publicity risks.
- Review of contributor agreements for clean assignment and enforceability.
- Title clearance for WE OUT HERE.
- Review of labor classification, payroll treatment, and any union implications.
- Review of final cut and marketing assets before public release or E&O placement.

## Working Legal Position

This memo is a production planning tool only and not final jurisdiction-specific legal advice. The actual legal risk profile depends on the final facts, script pages, design execution, contracts, and release strategy.
