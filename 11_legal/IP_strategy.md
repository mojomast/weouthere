# 11_legal/IP_strategy.md

## Purpose

Set the practical intellectual property strategy for WE OUT HERE so the project protects its own original assets, avoids avoidable infringement risk, and builds a usable record for development, production, marketing, and distribution. This is a business-and-creative planning document, not final legal advice.

## Core IP Position

WE OUT HERE should be treated as a package of related IP, not just a script.

The production should track and protect at least these categories:

- underlying series concept and characters
- pilot and episode scripts
- series bible and world-building materials
- original dialogue, jokes, and recurring bits
- visual identity, key art, and logo if adopted
- fictional in-show platform names, graphics, and UI treatments
- trailer, promo, clips, and social assets
- website copy, behind-the-scenes materials, and press kit assets

## Plain-Language Goal

Own what the team creates. Avoid borrowing what the team does not own. Do not build the show's identity around someone else's brand.

## Copyright Strategy

### What should be owned cleanly

- Script drafts and final scripts
- Bible and series materials
- Character bios and recurring story documents
- Original graphic assets
- Original fictional app or platform graphics used on screen
- Original music and sound design if commissioned
- Marketing copy, stills, posters, and trailers

### Recommended actions

1. Make sure all contributors assign rights in writing.
2. Keep dated versions of major creative materials.
3. Identify which materials are worth separate registration once they are stable.
4. Keep marketing vendors under assignment language too.

### Registration notes

- Counsel should advise what to register, when to register it, and in whose name.
- In many cases, core script materials, bible materials, artwork, or audiovisual works may be candidates for registration once fixed and stable enough.
- Registration timing can matter for enforcement and damages, so do not treat it as a pure administrative afterthought.

## Trademark And Title Strategy

### Title use

WE OUT HERE is the working project title and may become a public-facing title if clearance supports that decision.

Important note:

- Entertainment titles are not automatically protectable in all ways or all categories.
- Title conflicts can still create practical problems even where rights are limited.
- A streamer, distributor, platform, or insurer may care about title confusion risk even if the legal analysis is nuanced.

### Brand system to consider

- Series title: WE OUT HERE
- Production company name: as determined by company documents
- Optional tagline: only after review
- Social handles and domain names: reserve early if commercially important

### Recommended actions

1. Run title and trademark clearance through counsel before public launch.
2. Avoid public merch, brand collaborations, or paid campaigns until the title path is checked.
3. If the title is clear enough to use but weak to protect, build secondary identifiers the project can own more strongly, such as logo treatments or recurring fictional terms.

## Fictional Platform Depiction Strategy

This project has a specific branding sensitivity.

WE OUT HERE may depict a fictional real-time chat or community platform, but the production should not depend on literal Discord branding or an imitation so close that viewers or platforms would reasonably think the show is endorsed by, affiliated with, or depicting the actual service.

### Creative guardrails

- Use a fictional platform name.
- Design original icons, UI panels, sound cues, terminology, and color combinations.
- Avoid copying recognizable layouts, server structures, notification styles, mascots, taglines, or login flows too closely.
- Avoid showing real trademarks on-screen unless specifically cleared and approved.
- If a script joke references a real platform by name, review whether the line is necessary, artistically important, or easily replaceable.

### Practical standard

If a frame could be mistaken for a screenshot from the real service, it probably needs redesign or review.

## Character And World IP

The team should document the original expressive elements that make the series distinct.

That includes:

- character names and bios
- recurring relationships and conflicts
- fictional organizations, channels, communities, slang, and bits
- original visual motifs and recurring props
- fictional platform names and features

This documentation matters because:

- it helps support ownership records
- it reduces accidental drift into third-party material
- it creates a basis for future licensing, marketing, or spin-off planning

## Publicity, Privacy, And Personality Rights

Even when a show is fictional, risks can appear if a character feels too close to a real identifiable person.

Practical rules:

- Do not base a character too narrowly on one recognizable real person without counsel review.
- Do not use a real person's photo, handle, messages, likeness, or biographical details without permission unless counsel confirms a safe path.
- Be careful with stories inspired by actual incidents in niche communities where identification risk is higher.

## Merch, Promo, And Digital Extensions

If the project expands into merch, live events, digital extras, or social campaigns, the IP scope widens quickly.

Before expanding, confirm:

- the title and logo are clear enough for those uses
- cast releases cover advertising and merch-related promotion
- vendor agreements assign poster, key art, and social creative
- any slogan or recurring phrase is checked for conflicts

## Practical Notes

- A good fictional brand can reduce both legal risk and creative dependency.
- The cheapest time to redesign a fictional interface is before production design is locked.
- It is easier to clear an original asset than to defend a close imitation.
- Domains and handles are not the same thing as trademark rights, but they still matter commercially.

## Attorney Review Flags

- Clearance of the title WE OUT HERE for entertainment, promotion, and any merchandise categories.
- Whether and when to file copyright or trademark applications, and in which entity name.
- Whether the fictional platform depiction creates trademark, trade dress, dilution, false endorsement, or unfair competition risk.
- Whether any character or storyline is close enough to a real person or community to create publicity, privacy, or defamation concerns.
- Whether contemplated promo, clips, memes, or social posts reuse third-party materials in ways that need licenses.

## Working Legal Position

This strategy is a practical development framework, not final jurisdiction-specific legal advice. Protection and infringement risk depend on actual assets, markets, territories, and use cases. Final filing, clearance, and enforcement decisions should be made with qualified counsel.
