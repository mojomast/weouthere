# 11_legal/fair_use_and_parody_guidance.md

## Purpose

Provide practical guidance on when the WE OUT HERE team should treat fair use, parody, or satire as a legal question requiring caution rather than as a casual creative assumption. This is internal guidance only, not final legal advice.

## Status
Draft v1 - internal guidance. Working draft only. Fair use and parody are fact-intensive legal defenses, not automatic permissions. Counsel review is required before relying on them in a final cut, trailer, key art, or major public release.

## Dependencies

- Current scripts and cuts
- List of any third-party materials the team wants to reference or include
- Marketing and promo concepts
- Counsel review for any use the team is not replacing or licensing

## Plain-Language Rule

"It is parody" is not a production shortcut.

If the project can avoid using third-party material and still achieve the joke or point, that is usually the safer production choice.

## Key Concepts

### Fair use

Fair use is a legal defense that may sometimes protect certain uses of copyrighted material depending on context. It is not a blanket right to copy material because the project is creative, funny, low-budget, or non-malicious.

### Parody

Parody usually means the new work comments on, critiques, or makes fun of the original work itself. If the borrowed material is used mainly to comment on something else, the argument may be weaker.

### Satire

Satire often comments on society, culture, or a broader subject using borrowed material. It can still sometimes be defensible, but it is often riskier than true parody because the borrowed work is not necessarily the target of the commentary.

## What This Means For WE OUT HERE

For this project, the most likely misuse of "parody" would be around platform depiction, screen graphics, or recognizable brand-adjacent references.

Example risk area:

- recreating a near-literal Discord interface and saying it is a joke about online communities

That may not be the best legal position if the show is not actually commenting on Discord itself in a focused way. It may be much safer to use an original fictional platform design that still tells the same story.

## Practical Decision Rules

Use these simple rules during development:

1. If you can invent an original version, do that.
2. If the real material is important to the meaning of the joke, flag it for review.
3. If the use depends on copying a recognizable visual identity, assume review is required.
4. If anyone says "fair use" as the entire analysis, stop and escalate.

## Lower-Risk And Higher-Risk Patterns

### Lower-risk patterns

- making up a fictional platform, app, logo, or post style from scratch
- using generalized references to online culture without copying specific content
- writing original jokes that evoke a category of behavior rather than reproducing protected content

### Higher-risk patterns

- using actual screenshots, posts, chat logs, usernames, or avatars
- recreating a real platform's interface closely
- quoting lyrics, poems, or extended text for comedic effect
- using copyrighted clips or stills because the scene is "transformative"
- making posters, key art, or trailers that lean on recognizable third-party imagery

## Questions To Ask Before Relying On Fair Use Or Parody

- What exact third-party material is being used?
- Why is that exact material needed instead of an original substitute?
- Is the work actually commenting on that original material, or just borrowing it?
- How much of the original is being used, and how recognizable is it?
- Could the use affect the market for the original or trigger a complaint regardless of ultimate legal merit?

## Practical Notes

- A good legal defense can still be expensive to defend.
- Platform and distributor relationships may matter even where an argument is colorable.
- E&O insurers and distributors may be less excited about "we think this is fair use" than the creative team is.
- For an indie scripted comedy, original substitutions are often the best mix of safety and speed.

## Attorney Review Flags

- Any plan to use actual screenshots, clips, posts, or branded interface elements.
- Any scene built around commentary on a real platform, company, celebrity, or work.
- Any trailer, key art, or social campaign using borrowed material under a supposed parody rationale.
- Any script quote, lyric use, or reproduced visual work not clearly licensed.

## Working Legal Position

This guidance is a risk-screening tool only and not final jurisdiction-specific legal advice. Fair use, parody, and satire are context-specific legal analyses that should be assessed by qualified counsel based on the actual material and final use.
